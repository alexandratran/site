I live a partially examined life.

(This phrase is attributed to Socrates' famous quote, and more so to this philosophy podcast that I love.)

I am and always have been a voracious reader—which is how I encountered the field of philosophy when I was younger. It started with an interest in primary religious texts, then apologetics, then philosophy of religion, then metaphysics, epistemology, and moral philosophy. Discovering philosophy as a discipline was one of my most mind-boggling experiences. Philosophers, it seemed, were asking the most fundamental and nagging questions: Do we have free will? Does God exist? What exists? How do we know things? What is the nature of consciousness? What should we do? What do we mean by "should"?

Philosophers were not only asking these questions, but writing and arguing for countless theories as possible solutions. Moreover, philosophy seemed to pervade every field of study: mathemathics, physics, art, astronomy, etc. Whatever questions were the hardest, most abstract, and most unanswerable in field X were just considered a topic of study in "philosophy of X".

While the idea of philosophy being "the study of unanswerable questions" renders philosophy one of the most vibrant, wide-reaching, and thought-provoking disciplines, it also renders academic philosophy a risky vocational pursuit. These days, there is no such thing as "professional philosophers" besides philosophy professors.

That's kind of why I halted my undergrad philosophy studies. There was also the issue of having a romanticized view of what academic philosophy would be like going into college.

Regardless, philosophy is still one of my highest and most enjoyable pursuits. It is something I've been primed to be unable to avoid: my politics, career choices, religious views, and so on, all stem from personal philosophical conclusions. There is a heavy dose of philosophical literature in my reading pattern. I highly enjoy reading and writing about philosophy of science, philosophy of computation, philosophy of mind, logic, moral philosophy—and much of it is relevant to both my personal growth and my studies in math and computer science.

Even though I'm not an academic philosopher, an academic understanding of philosophy enriches my conversations, research, relationships, and worldview.
