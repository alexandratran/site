A blockchain is a cryptographically linked list of “blocks”, which contain transaction data. It is the ledger, the backbone, of most cryptocurrencies, and is maintained by a decentralized, peer-to-peer network, which is governed by a consensus protocol.

I like blockchain technology because it turns our traditional assumptions about trust and authority upside down and is a tool to undermine inadequate, centralized, locked-in infrastructure (think banks, supply chains, energy distribution, etc.) in a provably secure manner.

Not all use cases call for a blockchain. Not all blockchains are equal. Not all cryptocurrencies use blockchains.

When I first started learning about Bitcoin in fall of 2016, something that kickstarted my interest in the computer science behind it was reading, from cover to cover, Mastering Bitcoin by Andreas Antonopoulos and Bitcoin and Cryptocurrency Technologies by a team at Princeton. I also went through Blockchain at Berkeley’s pilot cryptocurrency course for undergrads, which I later helped lead.
